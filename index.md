---
title: Bilateral hemispheric coupling is widespread but dissociated from choice encoding across the mouse brain
abstract: |
    Hemispheric interactions during cognition reflect the division and integration of neural resources required to transform perception into action. Using the IBL Brainwidemap — large-scale Neuropixels recordings from mice performing a visual decision-making task — we characterize interhemispheric coordination across 39 brain regions. Cross-validated temporal Canonical Correlation Analysis reveals that bilateral coupling is brain-wide (r = 0.25–0.96) but spatially dissociated from choice encoding: hippocampal regions show the strongest interhemispheric synchrony yet the lowest decision content, while prefrontal cortex devotes over 20% of its bilateral coupling to the upcoming choice. Cross-correlogram analysis identifies three mechanistically distinct coupling regimes. Independent population decoders confirm symmetric bilateral encoding with no hemispheric lateralization. These results establish that bilateral coupling and decision-specific coordination are governed by distinct organizational principles across the mouse brain.

# Description

Most neuroscience research treats the two hemispheres independently, yet animals must coordinate bilateral neural activity to transform perception into action. While hemispheric specialization is well-documented in humans, rodent research has largely overlooked interhemispheric dynamics, risking the loss of crucial asymmetries in neural processing that contribute to cognitive function @mundorfLookingBothSides2025. Recent work has shown that functional lateralization may arise from learning-dependent synaptic plasticity producing asymmetric patterns of cross-hemispheric communication rather than being a fixed anatomical property @ocklenburgCrossHemisphericCommunication2024. Here we ask: is bilateral neural coupling during decision-making uniform across the brain, and does stronger coupling imply stronger decision encoding?

We analyzed simultaneous bilateral Neuropixels recordings from the International Brain Laboratory's Brainwidemap, comprising 152 bilateral region-pairs across 39 brain regions in mice performing a visual contrast discrimination task. Spike times were binned at 25 ms resolution and smoothed with a Gaussian kernel (σ ≈ 37 ms). Principal Component Analysis reduced population activity to three components per hemisphere, capturing a median of 25.9% of total variance (IQR: 19.6–34.0%) across 614 region-hemisphere entries (median 42 neurons per entry). While this represents a fraction of total variance, the leading components capture the dominant shared population dynamics most relevant to bilateral coupling, consistent with low-rank communication subspace approaches @semedoCorticalAreasInteract2019.

We applied temporal Canonical Correlation Analysis (CCA) independently at each of 80 time bins with cross-validation on trials. This per-bin approach is robust to temporal misalignments from spike sorting and to differences in circuit-specific timescales, ensuring that regional differences in coupling reflect genuine interhemispheric coordination rather than biophysical confounds. Peak bilateral coupling (r_peak) was identified as the maximum test-fold canonical correlation across all time bins. Within-hemisphere decision encoding was independently quantified as the Fréchet distance between choice-averaged PCA trajectories. Significance was assessed via 500 permutations of right-hemisphere trial order.

All 39 regions showed bilateral coupling significantly above permutation null, ranging from r = 0.25 (VISpm2/3) to r = 0.96 (subiculum) (@figure-main A). The strongest coupling was observed in hippocampal formation (SUB: 0.96; CA1: 0.91; DG: 0.85–0.88) and midbrain nuclei (APN: 0.89; MRN: 0.87), while higher visual areas showed the weakest. This brain-wide heterogeneity argues against a single global synchronization mechanism and extends the distributed coding framework @steinmetzDistributedCoding2019 to the interhemispheric domain.

Critically, peak coupling and choice encoding were uncorrelated across regions (Spearman ρ = −0.21, p = 0.17; @figure-main B). Hippocampal regions had the highest coupling but the lowest decision fraction — defined as |r_choice| / r_peak, the proportion of bilateral coupling devoted to choice — with CA1 at 0.04 and SUB at 0.06, meaning less than 6% of their bilateral synchrony related to the animal's upcoming choice. In contrast, prelimbic and infralimbic cortex had moderate coupling with the highest decision fraction (PL6a: 0.22; ILA6a: 0.20), indicating that over a fifth of their interhemispheric coordination specifically carried decision information. The Swanson flatmap projection confirmed this spatial dissociation across the brain (@figure-main C). This pattern parallels the selective communication subspace framework @semedoCorticalAreasInteract2019 extended to the bilateral context: not all shared variance constitutes communication.

Choice encoding showed a distinct temporal hierarchy. The strongest encoders were deep superior colliculus (SCdg: r_choice = 0.34, peak at +75 ms), prelimbic cortex (PL6a: 0.26, −75 ms), and posterior thalamus (PO: 0.26, +475 ms). PL6a's pre-movement timing supports motor planning, while PO and ILA6a peaked post-movement, consistent with outcome evaluation. Bilateral coupling was sustained across trial phases in motor, hippocampal, and midbrain regions, with phase-dependent modulation in visual and thalamic regions.

Cross-correlogram analysis (±500 ms lags, 80 time bins) revealed three temporal coupling structures (@figure-main D). Hippocampal and midbrain regions exhibited sharp zero-lag peaks flanked by negative correlations at ±100–150 ms, consistent with bilateral theta-band coherence. Superior colliculus and prefrontal regions showed broad sustained zero-lag coupling without oscillatory flanking, indicating tonic bilateral co-activation. A subset of association regions (RSPd6a, LP) displayed asymmetric lag profiles shifted 200–400 ms toward positive lags. No systematic hemispheric lead/lag was detected across the majority of regions, supporting a common-input model of bilateral coordination.

To verify that symmetric bilateral encoding was not an artifact of CCA's inherent symmetry constraint, an independent population decoder (logistic regression, 5-fold cross-validation) was trained per hemisphere. Across 67 bilaterally sampled regions, both hemispheres decoded choice with comparable accuracy (mean |AUC difference| = 0.05), with no region showing statistically significant lateralization. The decoder also revealed that hippocampal regions decode choice at above-chance levels in each hemisphere individually (CA1: 0.65; SUB: 0.66), despite minimal choice content in the bilaterally shared subspace — indicating that hippocampal hemispheres encode choice through dimensions not coordinated between hemispheres.

Plotting within-hemisphere decision strength (Fréchet distance) against between-hemisphere coupling reveals a 2D organizational space with four functional quadrants: strong encoding with strong coupling (PL6a, ILA6a, PO), strong coupling with weak shared encoding (hippocampal formation), strong encoding with independent hemispheres (MOs5, MOs6a), and weak both (@figure-main E). This taxonomy distinguishes regions where bilateral coordination is the mechanism of decision encoding from regions where coupling exists for other functional purposes.

Together, these results demonstrate that bilateral neural coupling during decision-making is brain-wide but functionally heterogeneous: hippocampal and midbrain circuits maintain oscillatory synchrony orthogonal to choice, while prefrontal and posterior thalamic regions use the bilateral subspace to encode the decision across distinct phases of motor planning and outcome evaluation. The organizational principle identified here — integration for motor output, segregation for sensory analysis — may reflect a conserved feature of bilateral brain organization with clinical relevance for predicting consequences of interhemispheric disconnection.
